\section*{Course Schedule}

\begin{description}

\item[09.04.24] \textbf{G-spaces, G-equivalences and the equivariant Whitehead theorem} \\
This talk develops the basic equivariant homotopy theory of $G$-spaces, for any (discrete) group $G$. In particular, definitions and basic properties of $G$-spaces and $G$-CW-complexes should be covered[cite: 141]. Both $G$-weak equivalences and $G$-homotopy equivalences should be explained[cite: 142]. The main result of the talk is the equivariant Whitehead theorem, saying that $G$-weak equivalences between $G$-CW-complexes are $G$-homotopy equivalences[cite: 143].

\item[16.04.24] \textbf{Reconstruction from fixed point data: Elmendorf's theorem} \\
\textit{Speaker:} K. Chen \\
The equivariant Whitehead theorem can be interpreted as saying that the collection of fixed points for all subgroups of $G$ determine the equivariant homotopy type[cite: 147]. Elmendorf's theorem makes a more precise statement how $G$-equivariant homotopy theory is equivalent to the homotopy theory of 'fixed point diagrams'[cite: 148]. This talk should explain and prove this theorem, and illustrate it by giving examples (universal spaces for families of subgroups, Eilenberg-MacLane spaces for coefficient systems)[cite: 149].

\item[23.04.24] \textbf{Equivariant orthogonal spectra} \\
\textit{Speaker:} T. Chatzidiamantis \\
There are different ways to set up a good theory of 'stable equivariant phenomena'; we will use the concept of orthogonal $G$-spectra[cite: 152, 153]. Besides the definition, this talk should explain equivariant stable homotopy groups of orthogonal $G$-spectra, equivariant stable equivalences, and present examples[cite: 154].

\item[30.04.24] \textbf{Equivariant homology theories} \\
\textit{Speaker:} K. Foth \\
This talk should discuss the suspension isomorphism (by a $G$-representation) for equivariant homotopy groups and the long exact mapping cone sequence arising from a morphism of orthogonal $G$-spectra[cite: 156]. Based on this, a proof should be given that every orthogonal $G$-spectrum defines an equivariant homology theory on the category of $G$-spaces[cite: 157]. An explanation in which way these homology theories are $RO(G)$-graded would be desirable[cite: 158].

\item[07.05.24] \textbf{The Wirthmüller isomorphism} \\
\textit{Speaker:} P. Srivastava \\
Let $H$ be a subgroup of a finite group $G$, and $Y$ an orthogonal $H$-spectrum[cite: 160]. The Wirthmüller isomorphism identifies the $G$-equivariant homotopy groups of the induced $G$-spectrum $G_{+}\wedge_{H}Y$ with the $H$-equivariant homotopy groups of $Y$[cite: 161].

\item[14.05.24] \textbf{Mackey functors: Restriction and transfer in equivariant homotopy groups} \\
\textit{Speaker:} E. Cortinovis \\
This talk should introduce the algebraic concept of a $G$-Mackey functor, ideally from two different perspectives (i.e., via 'generators and relations' and as additive functors on spans of finite $G$-sets)[cite: 165]. Then restriction and transfer maps should be defined for the equivariant homotopy groups of orthogonal $G$-spectra; time permitting, a proof of the Mackey functor properties should be sketched[cite: 166].

\item[28.05.24] \textbf{The tom Dieck splitting} \\
\textit{Speaker:} C. Liu \\
The tom Dieck splitting expresses the equivariant homotopy groups of a suspension spectrum of a $G$-space $X$ as a direct sum, indexed by conjugacy classes of subgroups $H$, of contributions coming from the $H$-fixed points $X^{H}$[cite: 169]. State and prove the tom Dieck splitting[cite: 170].

\item[04.06.24] \textbf{The equivariant zero stem} \\
\textit{Speaker:} B. Mandal \\
Establish the isomorphism between the Burnside ring of a finite group $G$ and the 0-th equivariant stable stem $\pi_{0}^{G}(\mathbb{S})$, given by the transfer maps in equivariant stable homotopy groups, originally due to Segal[cite: 173]. Show that the isomorphism is compatible with restriction and transfers. Describe explicit representatives for the transfer classes $tr_{H}^{G}(1)$[cite: 174]. Define the degree homomorphism into the ring of integral class functions and prove the congruences that characterize the image[cite: 176].

\item[11.06.24] \textbf{Geometric fixed points} \\
\textit{Speaker:} J. Zhang \\
Define the geometric fixed point spectra and the geometric fixed point map[cite: 179]. Identify geometric fixed point homotopy groups with equivariant homotopy groups of the smash product with the universal space for proper subgroups[cite: 180]. Show that geometric fixed point homotopy groups coincide with the localization of genuine equivariant homotopy groups at the Euler classes of orthogonal representations[cite: 181].

\item[18.06.24] \textbf{Equivariant Thom spectra} \\
\textit{Speaker:} Y. Song \\
There are different equivariant forms of the Thom spectrum whose homotopy groups are the unoriented bordism groups[cite: 185]. Discuss the $G$-equivariant orthogonal spectra $mo$ and $MO$[cite: 186]. Calculate the geometric fixed points of $mo$ and $MO$ in terms of non-equivariant bordism groups of Grassmannians[cite: 187]. Show that the equivariant homology theory represented by $MO$ is a localization of that represented by $mo$, obtained by inverting the 'inverse Thom classes'[cite: 188].

\item[25.06.24] \textbf{Equivariant bordism} \\
\textit{Speaker:} F. Neugebauer \\
Define the bordism groups of smooth closed $G$-manifolds, and explain how and why they form an equivariant homology theory[cite: 192]. Define the equivariant Thom-Pontryagin map from the $G$-bordism theory to the $G$-homology theory represented by $mo$, and show that it is an isomorphism for all finite groups $G$[cite: 193].

\item[09.07.24] \textbf{Higher categorical description of genuine G-spectra} \\
\textit{Speaker:} L. Michele \\
Explain the higher categorical description of the $\infty$-category of genuine $G$-spectra as the localization of the $\infty$-category of $G$-spaces by inverting representation spheres[cite: 196]. At least the 'additive' version should be covered, ideally also the 'multiplicative' version[cite: 197]. This talk differs form the other ones in that basic knowledge about higher category theory is essential[cite: 198].

\end{description}
